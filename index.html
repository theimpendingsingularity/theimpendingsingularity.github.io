The Impending Singularity:

Implications of the Past, Present, and Future

This page is dedicated to providing an in-depth history of the theories behind the Technological Singularity and also my own input on how the power of hacking may exacerbate the dangers of Singularity once it has been achieved.

created by Mike Chen




What is the Technological Singularity?

"a hypothetical event in which artificial intelligence would be capable of recursive self-improvement (progressively redesigning itself), or of autonomously building ever smarter and more powerful machines than itself, up to the point of a runaway effect--an intelligence explosion that yields an intelligence surpassing all current human control or understanding"[1]



Theorists Behind the Technological Singularity

Nicolas de Condorcet (born in 1794) is an 18th century philosopher, mathematician, and revolutionary, and is one of the first to ever contemplate the existence of a singularity. He states in his 1794 Sketch for a Historical Picture of the Progress of the Human Mind,

"Nature has set no term to the perfection of human faculties; that the perfectibility of man is truly indefinite; and that the progress of this perfectibility, from now onwards independent of any power that might wish to halt it, has no other limit than the duration of the globe upon which nature has cast us. This progress will doubtless vary in speed, but it will never be reversed as long as the earth occupies its present place in the system of the universe, and as long as the general laws of this system produce neither a general cataclysm nor such changes as will deprive the human race of its present faculties and its present resources."
Stanislaw Ulam is reportedly the first person to use the word" singularity" in the context of technological singularity in his obituary in 1958 for John von Neumann, a Hungarian-American mathematician, in which he states that the "ever accelerating progress of technology and changes in the mode of human life, which gives the appearance of approaching some essential singularity in the history of the race beyond which human affairs, as we know them, could not continue."
I.J. Good (born in 1916) was the founding father of the term "intelligence explosion." The "intelligence explosion" hypothesizes that if machines could exceed human intellect by just even a little bit, machines would be able to regenerate and improve themselves to get smarter. After several waves of regenerations, machines will reach a state of superintelligence.
Vernor Vinge (born in 1944) coined the term Technological Singularity in his 1993 essay "The Coming Technological Singularity: How to Survive in the Post-Human Era," in which he predicts that within 30 years (by 2023), humans "will have the technological means to create superhuman intelligence" and that "shortly after, the human era will be ended."

He predicts that this will happen in 4 ways:
1. the development of computers that are "awake" and superhumanly intelligent
2. large computer networks (and their associated users) may "wake up" as a superhumanly entity
3. computer/human interfaces may become so intimate that users may reasonably be considered superhumanly intelligent
4. biological science may find ways to improve upon the natural human intellect
Ray Kurzweil (born in 1948) is an American writer, computer scientist, inventor, and futurist. In his 2006 non-fiction book The Singularity is Near: When Humans Transcend Biology, he predicts that the law of accelerating returns will allow technologoical growth to achieve Singularity by year 2045, which is a point in time where humans will stop being able to comprehend it.

In 2011, he reinforced his previous 2006 notion that the Singularity would occur by 2045, as he told Times magazine: "We will successfully reverse-engineer the human brain by the mid-2020s. By the end of that decade, computers will be capable of human-level intelligence."
Aubrey de Grey (born in 1963) is an English writer and biomedical gerontologist (an expert in the human biological aging process). In 2010, de Grey used the term "Methuselarity" to describe the effects of the technological singularity on biomedicine. "Methuselarity" is a point at which medical technology advances so rapidly that the expected human lifespan increases over 1 year per year.

Potential Dangers of the Singularity

Devolution of the Human Race


humans would start relinquishing tasks to artifical intelligence



A Post-Human Era


ultra-intelligent robots would reproduce even more intelligent robots that would replace the need for a human race



A Destruction of the World, And Potentially Universe


once the human race is exterminated, a war between robots (who most likely would produce unlimited weapons of mass destruction) could destroy everything



Hackers of the Singularity


1) humans could hack into robotic technology, using them for good or evil
2) superintelligent robots will have a conscious of their own and will be able to hack inferior robots for good or evil
